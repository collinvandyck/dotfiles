# The Elements of Style (1918) — Condensed

_Public domain text by William Strunk Jr. Condensed for quick reference: each rule keeps its statement, the nuances that change an edit, and one example._

## II. Elementary Rules of Usage

### Rule 1. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's.

Whatever the final consonant: *Charles's friend*, *the witch's malice*. Exceptions: ancient names in *-es/-is* (*Moses' laws*), *Jesus'*, and *for conscience' sake*. The pronominal possessives *hers, its, theirs, yours, oneself* take no apostrophe.

### Rule 2. In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a comma after each term except the last.

*red, white, and blue* — *He opened the letter, read it, and made a note of its contents.* (In firm names the last comma is omitted: *Brown, Shipley & Co.*)

### Rule 3. Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas.

*The best way to see a country, unless you are pressed for time, is to travel on foot.* Never insert one comma and omit the other. If a conjunction precedes the expression, the first comma goes before the conjunction: *He saw us coming, and unaware that we had learned of his treachery, greeted us with a smile.*

Non-restrictive clauses (which merely add information) are set off; restrictive clauses (which identify *which one*) are not:

- Non-restrictive: *The audience, which had at first been indifferent, became more and more interested.*
- Restrictive: *The candidate who best meets these requirements will obtain the place.*

Always parenthetic: the year in a date, *etc.* and *jr.*

### Rule 4. Place a comma before a conjunction introducing a co-ordinate clause.

*The early records of the city have disappeared, and the story of its first years can no longer be reconstructed.* Often the relation is sharper if the first clause is made subordinate: *As the early records of the city have disappeared, the story of its first years can no longer be reconstructed.* Same applies to *as, for, or, nor, while*. When the subject is shared and stated once, keep the comma before *but* but drop it before *and*: *I have heard his arguments, but am still unconvinced.*

### Rule 5. Do not join independent clauses by a comma.

Use a semicolon (or a period, or a comma + conjunction): *Stevenson's romances are entertaining; they are full of exciting adventures.* The semicolon holds even when the second clause opens with an adverb (*accordingly, besides, then, therefore, thus*). Exception: very short, parallel clauses — *Man proposes, God disposes.*

### Rule 6. Do not break sentences in two.

Do not use a period for a comma. Wrong: *He was an interesting talker. A man who had traveled all over the world.* The first period should be a comma. (An emphatic fragment is allowed when the emphasis is clearly intended: *Again and again he called out. No reply.*)

### Rule 7. A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject.

*Walking slowly down the road, he saw a woman.* — Wrong: *Being in a dilapidated condition, I was able to buy the house very cheap.* The same applies to opening appositives, adjectives, and phrases: *On arriving in Chicago, his friends met him at the station* → *When he arrived in Chicago, his friends met him at the station.*

## III. Elementary Principles of Composition

### Rule 8. Make the paragraph the unit of composition: one paragraph to each topic.

Each topic gets its own paragraph; the break signals a new step to the reader. Single-sentence paragraphs are for transitions or dialogue. In dialogue, a new paragraph begins with each change of speaker.

### Rule 9. As a rule, begin each paragraph with a topic sentence; end it in conformity with the beginning.

The reader should grasp the paragraph's purpose as he begins and retain it as he ends. The usual pattern: (a) topic sentence at or near the start; (b) sentences that develop it; (c) a final sentence that emphasizes the thought or states a consequence. Avoid ending on a digression or trivial detail. In narrative, the opening sentence often just names what the paragraph concerns.

### Rule 10. Use the active voice.

More direct and vigorous: *I shall always remember my first visit to Boston* beats *My first visit to Boston will always be remembered by me.* The passive is sometimes needed (when its object should be the subject), but prefer the active. Replace perfunctory *there is* / *could be heard* with a real verb: *There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground* → *Dead leaves covered the ground.* Avoid making one passive depend on another.

### Rule 11. Put statements in positive form.

Make definite assertions; avoid colorless, hesitating, non-committal language. Use *not* for denial or antithesis, never for evasion.

- *He was not very often on time* → *He usually came late.*
- *not honest* → *dishonest*; *did not remember* → *forgot*; *did not pay any attention to* → *ignored.*

Antithesis is strong: *Not charity, but simple justice.*

### Rule 12. Use definite, specific, concrete language.

Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract.

- *A period of unfavorable weather set in* → *It rained every day for a week.*
- *He showed satisfaction as he took possession of his well-earned reward* → *He grinned as he pocketed the coin.*

### Rule 13. Omit needless words.

Vigorous writing is concise. Make every word tell.

- *the question as to whether* → *whether*
- *there is no doubt but that* → *no doubt*
- *he is a man who* → *he*
- *in a hasty manner* → *hastily*
- *owing to the fact that* → *since*; *the fact that he had not succeeded* → *his failure*

Revise *the fact that* out of every sentence. *Who is* / *which was* are often superfluous: *His brother, who is a member of the same firm* → *His brother, a member of the same firm.* Combine a single idea dribbled across many clauses into one sentence.

### Rule 14. Avoid a succession of loose sentences.

Especially the two-clause kind joined by *and, but, so, who, which, when, where, while*. A single one is fine; a string of them becomes monotonous and sing-song. Recast some into simple sentences, semicolon-joined clauses, or periodic sentences.

### Rule 15. Express co-ordinate ideas in similar form.

Parallel construction: similar content gets similar form.

- *Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method, while now the laboratory method is employed* → *Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method; now it is taught by the laboratory method.*

An article or preposition applying to all members of a series is used once before the first or repeated before each. Correlatives (*both/and*, *not only/but also*, *either/or*) must be followed by the same construction: *It was both a long ceremony and very tedious* → *The ceremony was both long and tedious.*

### Rule 16. Keep related words together.

Word position shows relationship. Don't separate subject and verb with a transferable phrase: *Wordsworth, in the fifth book of The Excursion, gives a minute description* → *In the fifth book of The Excursion, Wordsworth gives a minute description.* Keep the relative pronoun next to its antecedent. Place modifiers next to the word they modify: *He only found two mistakes* → *He found only two mistakes.*

### Rule 17. In summaries, keep to one tense.

Summarize a drama in the present tense; a poem/story/novel preferably in the present. Antecedent action takes the perfect (if present) or past perfect (if past). Don't shift tenses. When summarizing someone's thought, say so once at the outset — don't repeat *he said*, *the author adds*.

### Rule 18. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end.

The most prominent spot is the end; the next is the beginning.

- *This steel is principally used for making razors, because of its hardness* → *Because of its hardness, this steel is principally used in making razors.*

The word entitled to the end is usually the logical predicate — the *new* element. To emphasize a subject, move it to the predicate's position: *Through the middle of the valley flowed a winding stream.*

## V. Words and Expressions Commonly Misused

| Word/phrase | Guidance |
| --- | --- |
| **All right** | Two words. Fine for "agreed"; otherwise avoid. |
| **As good or better than** | Recast: *as good as his, or better.* |
| **As to whether** | *Whether* suffices. |
| **Bid** | Takes the infinitive without *to*; past tense (= ordered) is *bade*. |
| **But** | Unnecessary after *doubt* and *help* (*no doubt that*, not *no doubt but that*). Don't follow one *but* with another. |
| **Can** | = *am able*. Not a substitute for *may* (permission). |
| **Case** | Usually unnecessary: *In many cases the rooms were poorly ventilated* → *Many of the rooms were poorly ventilated.* |
| **Certainly** | Don't sprinkle it in to intensify everything (cf. *very*). |
| **Character** | Redundant: *Acts of a hostile character* → *Hostile acts.* |
| **Claim (vb.)** | = *lay claim to*. Not a substitute for *declare, maintain, charge*. |
| **Clever** | Overused; restrict to ingenuity in small matters. |
| **Compare** | *Compare to* = note resemblance (different orders); *compare with* = note difference (same order). |
| **Consider** | No *as* when it means "believe to be": *I consider him competent.* |
| **Data** | Plural (like *phenomena*): *These data were tabulated.* |
| **Dependable** | Needless substitute for *reliable, trustworthy*. |
| **Different than** | Use *different from*. |
| **Divided into** | Not *composed of*. Poems are composed of stanzas; plays are divided into acts. |
| **Don't** | = *do not*. The contraction of *does not* is *doesn't*. |
| **Due to** | Wrong in adverbial phrases (*due to carelessness*); use *because of/owing to*. Correct when tied to a noun: *losses due to fire.* |
| **Effect** | Noun = result; verb = bring about (not *affect* = influence). Avoid vague "an Oriental effect." |
| **Etc.** | = *and the rest*. Don't use if the reader is left in doubt; never after *such as* or *for example*. |
| **Fact** | Only for matters open to direct verification, not judgments. |
| **Factor** | Hackneyed: *the great factor in his winning* → *He won by being better trained.* |
| **Feature** | Hackneyed; usually adds nothing. Avoid the advertising verb sense. |
| **Fix** | Colloquial for *arrange/mend*; in writing use *fasten, make firm*. |
| **Folk** | Collective = *people*; use the singular form. |
| **Get** | Avoid *have got* for *have*. Participle is *got*. |
| **He is a man who** | Redundant: *He is a man who is very ambitious* → *He is very ambitious.* |
| **However** | Meaning *nevertheless*, don't put it first: *At last, however, we succeeded.* First-position *however* = *in whatever way*. |
| **Interesting** | Don't announce it; make it so. Drop "An interesting story is told of." |
| **Kind of / sort of** | Not for *rather* (*kind of good*); restrict to literal sense (*a kind of resin*). |
| **Less** | For number use *fewer*: *He had fewer men.* *Less* = quantity. |
| **Like** | Not for *as*. *Like* governs nouns/pronouns; before clauses use *as*: *He thought as I did.* |
| **Line, along these lines** | Overworked; discard. *Spoke along the same lines* → *spoke to the same effect.* |
| **Literal, literally** | Don't use to prop up a metaphor: *literally dead with fatigue* → *almost dead.* |
| **Lose out** | Less emphatic than *lose*; likewise *try out, win out, sign up*. |
| **Most** | Not for *almost*: *Most everybody* → *Almost everybody.* |
| **Nature** | Redundant like *character*: *acts of a hostile nature* → *hostile acts.* |
| **Near by** | Prefer *near* or *near at hand*. As adjective use *neighboring*. |
| **Oftentimes** | Archaic; use *often*. |
| **One hundred and one** | Keep the *and*. |
| **One of the most** | Threadbare opener; avoid. Verb in the following clause is plural: *one of the ablest men that have…* |
| **Participle for verbal noun** | Use the possessive before a gerund: *Do you mind my asking* (not *me asking*). |
| **People** | Political term; not interchangeable with *the public*. |
| **Phase** | A stage of development, not *aspect* or *topic*. |
| **Possess** | Not a fancy substitute for *have/own*: *He possessed great courage* → *He had great courage.* |
| **Prove** | Past participle is *proved*. |
| **Respective, respectively** | Usually omit: *listed under the names of their respective authors* → *…of their authors.* |
| **Shall, will** | Future: *shall* (1st person), *will* (2nd/3rd). *I will* = determination/consent. |
| **So** | Don't use as an intensifier in writing (*so good*). On *so* introducing clauses, see Rule 4. |
| **Split infinitive** | Avoided by careful writers: *to diligently inquire* → *to inquire diligently.* |
| **State** | Not a mere substitute for *say*; = express fully (*state his objections*). |
| **Student body** | Awkward; use *students*. |
| **System** | Often needless: *the dormitory system* → *dormitories.* |
| **Thanking you in advance** | Avoid; write *Will you please* or *I shall be obliged.* |
| **They** | Singular antecedents (*each, everybody, anybody*) take a singular pronoun. |
| **Very** | Use sparingly; prefer words strong in themselves. |
| **Viewpoint** | Write *point of view*; don't misuse for *view* or *opinion*. |
| **While** | Not for *and/but/although* indiscriminately; use it literally (= *during the time that*). Replace the loose connective with a semicolon. |
| **Whom** | Don't use for *who* when it's the subject of a following verb: *who he said would send the money.* |
| **Worth while** | Overworked; strictly applies to actions. *A worth while story* is indefensible. |
| **Would** | First-person conditional takes *should*: *I should not have succeeded without his help.* For habitual past action, drop *would*: *Once a year he visited the old mansion.* |
